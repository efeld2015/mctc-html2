History

The Camino de Santiago, or the Way of St. James in English, is a network of pilgrims' ways or pilgrimages leading to the shrine of the apostle James in the cathedral of Santiago de Compostela in Galicia in northwestern Spain, where tradition holds that the remains of the apostle are buried. In this article, I will give a brief overview of this ancient pathway.

Routes

The Camino has various starting points to choose from.

The most popular routes are:

  • Camino Frances (Northern Spain)
  • Camino Portugues (Along the Portuguese coastline)
  • Camino del Norte
  • Camino Primitivo (the oldest of the routes)
  • Camino Ingles (named as such because it was traditionally the preferred route for English and Irish pilgrims)

    • Other routes start as far away as Italy and France.

    Reasons to Walk

    The reasons why modern pilgrims walk the Camino ancient trail has evolved from its religious roots, and it is now a rewarding experience for anyone who enjoys walking or cycling.

    You will experience many things along the way, including:

  • Various cultures
  • Historic sites and monuments
  • Varied landscapes travelling across the country, from coastal Camino routes to vineyards, mountains, or small towns.
  • Meeting people from all over the world who are also walking the Camino de Santiago.
    • Distance

    The beauty of the Camino routes is that you can walk as much or as little as you want; you could spend weeks walking from Le Puy to Santiago, one month walking from St Jean Pied de Port, a week or just a few days.

    There is a Camino route for everyone but the one thing to keep in mind is that in order to receive your Compostela pilgrim certificate, you will need to walk at least the last 100km into Santiago de Compostela or cycle the last 200km.
